1. A 45-year-old man with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back is diagnosed with acute pancreatitis. Which anatomical structure lies posterior to the body of the pancreas and is commonly involved in pancreatic inflammation?

2. A 60-year-old smoker presents with hoarseness and left-sided chest pain. A chest X-ray shows a left upper lobe mass. Which nerve is most likely compressed by this mass, causing the hoarseness?
A) Phrenic nerve
B) Vagus nerve
C) Recurrent laryngeal nerve
D) Sympathetic chain
E) Accessory nerve

Correct Answer: C
Explanation: The left recurrent laryngeal nerve loops under the aortic arch and is vulnerable to left lung apical (lung cancer) lesions. Compression causes vocal cord paralysis → hoarseness.

3. A young man sustains a stab wound to the right 5th intercostal space at the midclavicular line. Which structure is most immediately at risk?
A) Right atrium
B) Right ventricle
C) Left ventricle
D) Superior vena cava
E) Aorta

Correct Answer: B
Explanation: The right ventricle forms most of the anterior cardiac surface and lies behind the 4th–6th intercostal spaces near the sternum. A midclavicular stab at 5th ICS often penetrates the right ventricle.

4. A patient with cirrhosis develops massive ascites. Paracentesis is planned. Which anatomical landmark is safest to avoid injury to the inferior epigastric artery?

B) 2 cm lateral to midline
C) Left lower quadrant lateral to rectus sheath
D) Directly through the linea alba
E) Right upper quadrant

Correct Answer: D
Explanation: The linea alba is avascular. The inferior epigastric artery runs lateral to the rectus abdominis. Midline puncture (linea alba) avoids vessels; lateral puncture risks inferior epigastric injury.

Explanation: The right atrium lies just deep to the 2nd–3rd intercostal spaces near the sternum. A stab there can cause cardiac tamponade presenting with hypotension and elevated JVP (Beck’s triad).
